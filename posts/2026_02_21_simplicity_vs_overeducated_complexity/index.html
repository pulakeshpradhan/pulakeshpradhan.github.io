Life is simple. Let’s keep it that way.

Education
Philosophy
Simplicity
Why the most ‘highly educated’ people often create the most uneducated complexities, and a reminder that life is fundamentally simple.
Author

Pulakesh Pradhan

Published

February 21, 2026

We live in a world that worships credentials. We chase degrees, certifications, and titles, believing they are the ultimate proof of our intelligence and worth. However, a strange paradox has emerged: many of the most highly educated individuals are the ones performing the most "uneducated" or unnecessarily complex actions.

The "Smart" Trap

Education is meant to broaden the mind, but for some, it serves as a toolkit for exploitation. Because they are "so-called smart," they believe they have the license to use others as stepping stones.

Instead of using their knowledge to simplify solutions or uplift people, they use it to: * Play office politics: Creating divisions instead of collaborations. * Navigate loopholes: Finding ways to exploit systems and people for personal gain. * Create complexity: Making simple tasks look difficult to justify their "expertise" or position.

This is the ultimate irony: the higher the education, the more "cluttered" the mind often becomes with the weight of ego and the desire for power.

Over-Educated Complexity vs. Basic Truths

I often observe "educated" people doing what I call "uneducated work." By this, I don’t mean manual labor or simple tasks—those are honorable. I mean the work of manipulation, the work of gossip, the work of building invisible walls between colleagues, and the work of making common sense seem like a rare commodity.

They use their vocabulary to hide their intentions and their degrees to shield themselves from accountability. They have learned how to think, but somewhere along the line, they forgot why we think: to solve problems, to help others, and to lead meaningful lives.

The Urban Paradox: Education Without Humanity

In our growing urban centers, we see the highest clusters of “educated” people. Skyscrapers are filled with professionals holding advanced degrees. Yet, these very areas often showcase the greatest loss of humanity.

We see: * Dirty Streets: People who can solve complex algorithms or manage multi-million dollar budgets, yet lack the basic civic sense to keep their own streets clean. * Indifference: A crowd of educated individuals walking past a person in need without a second glance. * Isolation: Living in high-rise apartments, surrounded by thousands of people, yet failing to know or care for their own neighbors.

It is as if the more “civilized” and “educated” we become, the more we detach from the fundamental human values of empathy, community, and respect for our environment. We build smart cities, but we forget how to be simple, decent humans.

Life is Fundamentally Simple

In most cases, the solutions to our biggest problems are not found in 500-page corporate strategies or complex political maneuvers. They are found in simple truths: * Honesty over politics. * Directness over manipulation. * Kindness over exploitation.

Life is very simple, yet we work incredibly hard—using our "education"—to make it difficult. We think that being smart means being complex. But true intelligence is the ability to take the complex and make it simple.

Conclusion

Education without character is just a tool for disaster. If our learning doesn’t lead us back to a life of sincerity and simplicity, then we have missed the point of being educated.

Let’s stop being "smart" in ways that exploit, and start being wise in ways that simplify. Life is simple. Let’s keep it that way.

What do you think? Have you noticed this paradox in your own professional or social circles?
